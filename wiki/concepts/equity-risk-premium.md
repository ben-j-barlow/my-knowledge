# Equity Risk Premium (ERP)

The excess return investors expect from holding equities over the risk-free rate. Commonly measured as the earnings yield of an index minus the yield on a comparable-duration government bond (typically the 10-year Treasury).

**Formula (simplified):**
```
ERP = Earnings Yield − Risk-Free Rate
    = (1 / P/E) − Treasury Yield
```

## Why It Matters

ERP is the compensation investors receive for taking equity risk over simply holding government bonds. When ERP is high, equities offer a meaningful premium and have a larger margin of safety. When ERP compresses toward zero or goes negative, equities offer little or no reward for additional risk relative to Treasuries.

## Current State (May 2026)

As of mid-May 2026, with the 10-year Treasury at ~4.6%:

- **S&P 500:** ERP near zero — barely any pickup vs. Treasuries
- **Nasdaq 100 / Information Technology / Semiconductors / Magnificent Seven:** ERP is negative — these indices and sectors are offering *less* in earnings yield than you can earn from a risk-free government bond

This does not guarantee a sharp near-term decline — overpriced markets can remain overpriced for months. However, it eliminates the margin of safety. Any negative catalyst (hotter inflation, a weak Treasury auction, a "good but not perfect" earnings report) has a much larger impact when there is no buffer.

## What Compresses ERP

1. **Rising Treasury yields** (risk-free rate rises without a compensating rise in earnings yield)
2. **Multiple expansion** (P/E rises without earnings growth, reducing earnings yield)
3. **Both simultaneously** — the situation in May 2026, driven by AI enthusiasm + inflation-related rate rise

## Connection to Equity Duration

ERP and [equity duration](equity-duration.md) compound each other's risk. A long-duration stock (most value in future cash flows) is already structurally vulnerable to rising discount rates. If that stock also has a compressed or negative ERP, it has no cushion. Small misses or macro surprises translate directly into price weakness.
