# US-China Tech Rivalry

The structural competition between the United States and China across semiconductor supply chains, AI development, rare earth materials, and military technology. A persistent source of market uncertainty that affects valuations across the entire AI infrastructure stack.

## Core Fault Lines

| Issue | Status (May 2026) |
|---|---|
| Taiwan | China maintains it is the "central issue"; US holds "strategic ambiguity"; no change |
| Rare earths | China controls ~60–80% of global rare earth processing; no export agreements reached |
| Semiconductors | US export controls on advanced chips/tools remain; no agreement on AI guardrails |
| Hormuz / Iran | China verbally signalled willingness to assist; no official confirmation |
| Military AI | No guardrails framework established |

## Trump-Xi Summit (May 2026)

Characterised by market participants as a major geopolitical stabilisation moment; in practice, it was a "cool-down" meeting that avoided further escalation without resolving any structural issue. Markets initially reacted positively to the optics; the author of the Agar Capital piece argues this was a misread of the summit's actual substance.

Outcomes of note: limited soybean and Boeing commercial agreements — insufficient to affect global capital costs, supply chain fragmentation risk, or Strait of Hormuz security.

## Market Implications

- **Semiconductor supply chain:** US export control licensing (replacing VEU program, September 2025) creates material annual uncertainty for Samsung and SK Hynix China operations
- **Rare earths:** Unresolved dependency affects defence, EV, and advanced electronics supply chains
- **Technology fragmentation:** Risk of parallel US and China tech stacks persists — affects hyperscaler strategies, chip design standards, and AI model governance
- **Investor uncertainty:** Unresolved geopolitical risk compresses valuation multiples and raises required equity risk premiums for exposed companies

## Relationship to the AI Supply Chain

The AI semiconductor supply chain is the most geopolitically exposed layer of the technology stack. TSMC in Taiwan, Korean HBM producers (SK Hynix, Samsung) operating China fabs under annual US export licences, and Chinese demand for Nvidia GPUs (blocked by export controls) all sit at the intersection of this rivalry.
