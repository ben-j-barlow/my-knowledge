# Equity Duration

An analogy between stocks and bonds: just as a bond's duration measures its sensitivity to interest rate changes, a stock's "equity duration" measures how much of its value is tied to distant future cash flows versus current earnings. The longer the equity duration, the more sensitive the stock is to changes in the discount rate.

## The Core Idea

When valuing a company via DCF, future cash flows are discounted back to present value using a rate that includes the risk-free rate (Treasury yields) and cost of capital. When Treasury yields rise, that discount rate rises — and the present value of distant cash flows falls disproportionately compared to near-term cash flows.

A stock that earns most of its value from cash flows 5–10+ years out is more vulnerable to discount rate increases than one earning most of its value today, even if both companies have identical growth trajectories.

## Short vs Long Duration Stocks

| Characteristic | Short Duration | Long Duration |
|---|---|---|
| Value source | Current profitability | Future projected earnings |
| Rate sensitivity | Low | High |
| Examples | Walmart, consumer staples, mature software | Nvidia, high-growth AI names, Mag 7 |
| Behaviour in rising-rate environment | Relatively resilient | Multiple compression even with good earnings |

## Why It Matters Now (May 2026)

With the 30-year Treasury above 5% and the 10-year at ~4.6%, the AI equity rally is concentrated in exactly the longest-duration names — companies whose valuations are heavily weighted toward future AI-driven cash flows. Good earnings reports do not insulate these stocks if the discount rate has risen: the same earnings are worth less when discounted at a higher rate.

The practical implication: a company like Nvidia can post excellent results and still see its stock pressured, simply because the cost of money has risen.

## Relationship to Equity Risk Premium

Equity duration and [equity risk premium](equity-risk-premium.md) interact: when ERP is already compressed to near zero or negative, a rising discount rate leaves almost no buffer. A stock with long equity duration and a near-zero ERP has a small margin of safety against any disappointment.
