# We're Missing Data: The Other Half of AI Transformation

> Source: https://ericdataproduct.substack.com/p/were-missing-data-the-other-half
> Author: Eric Weber
> Published: 2026-05-03

## Summary

AI transformation has two halves: technical (tools, eval infra, agent frameworks) and operating (manager redesign, career architecture, team composition, stakeholder trust). These multiply rather than add. Most organizations are funding the technical half deliberately and leaving the operating half to drift — which is why the productivity bump plateaus. The teams doing this well are funding both stacks on purpose.

## Key Claims

### The Two-Stack Model

> "I think these two halves multiply rather than add."

- **Technical stack**: model providers, eval infrastructure, agent frameworks, applications — well-funded, extensively written about, "comparatively easy to know what to do about"
- **Operating stack**: manager redesign, career architecture, team composition, data-product partnership, stakeholder trust, communication norms — "almost nobody is funding deliberately"

A serious technical investment + stuck operating model = productivity bump then plateau  
A modest technical investment + updated operating model = lower initial throughput but greater compounding capability over time

### Operating Transformation Components

**Manager redesign**: manager-as-router (assign tickets, unblock, run standups) is dissolving → manager-as-coach (develop judgment, set quality bars, calibrate AI output review). People who were excellent managers under the old model often struggle under the new one without support.

**Career architecture**: the IC ladder built around "associate → senior → staff" with "data scientist" or "ML engineer" titles no longer maps to the actual work. Three distinct job shapes now: AI output curators, algorithm/primitive writers, end-to-end product orchestrators.

**Team composition**: most teams are sized as if everyone produces work the same way. High-performing teams have rebalanced: more people on data, evaluation, and trust functions; fewer on pure feature execution. Most companies treat this as headcount drama; a few treat it as a deliberate operating choice.

**Data-product partnership**: PMs can now generate first-pass analyses in ~15 minutes with Claude or ChatGPT. The data function's value has shifted from being the source of analyses to: defining what analyses should mean, calibrating evidence quality, and building measurement infrastructure the product team uses. Both sides are "operating on a contract that is no longer current."

**Stakeholder trust**: with AI in the loop, throughput is a noisy signal. Teams that handle this invest in shorter feedback loops, visible decisions, and plain explanations of what changed and why. Otherwise stakeholders quietly conclude the team is less valuable, even when it is more valuable.

**Communication norms**: "We ran an analysis" is no longer sufficient — anyone with a Claude window can say that. What lands: "we made this decision based on this evidence and here is what we expect to see." The shift sounds small; it redesigns the team's relationship to the rest of the company.

### What Leaders Should Do

Stop treating AI transformation as a technical investment with change-management bolted on at the end. Budget for both stacks. Measure both. Hold leaders accountable for both.

> "If your engineering or data org has a multi-million-dollar AI tooling budget and no equivalent budget for manager redesign, career architecture, team rebalancing, trust mechanics, and communication norm updates, you are funding one half of the transformation and hoping the other half happens for free."
