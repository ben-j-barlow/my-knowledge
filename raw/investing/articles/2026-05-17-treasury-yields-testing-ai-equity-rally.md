# Treasury Yields Are Testing The AI Equity Rally

**Author:** Agar Capital  
**Published:** May 17, 2026  
**Source:** Seeking Alpha  
**Tickers:** SPX, SP500, SPY, IVW, NDX, QQQ, SPHQ, SPYG, DJI, TLT, SGOV

---

## Summary

- Long-term yields are now the main threat to U.S. equities, with the 30-year Treasury above 5% and the 10-year near 4.6%, forcing investors to rethink stretched valuations.
- The Trump-Xi summit failed to deliver concrete progress on Taiwan, rare earths, semiconductors, Iran, or the Strait of Hormuz, leaving geopolitical and inflation risks unresolved.
- I am reducing equity exposure from 100% to 75%, cutting high-beta technology, especially semiconductors and memory, while increasing healthcare, pharma, and selected low-P/E consumer staples.

---

## Executive Summary

For a lot of the week equity markets have shown incredible strength as they continue to hold their ground against rising interest rates in Treasuries, increasing oil prices, and an increase in inflation concerns. The S&P 500 and Nasdaq 100 are trading at all time highs due to very good earnings reports from big cap companies with growth potential, artificial intelligence (AI) optimism, and strong demand for large capitalization stocks. However, that strength can create a huge problem with regard to pricing.

U.S. Treasury Yields climbed back into the 4.5%–4.6% level on the 10 year Treasury Yield and above 5% for the 30 year Treasury Yield. That is significant because longer term U.S. Treasury Yields lower the present value of future cash flows. More importantly, many of today's stock valuations rely heavily on projected cash flows in years to come. Therefore, when U.S. Treasury yields rise, the equity risk premium can compress unless earnings yields rise as well. In practice, that often means lower valuation multiples, especially for long-duration growth stocks. When the premium goes down, if the multiple remains high, then there is less room for error before you experience a pullback in your stock investments.

In addition, although the Trump-Xi meeting was productive regarding certain issues, the two leaders were unable to make significant progress in regard to many issues such as Taiwan, rare earths, semiconductors and the Iran/Hormuz issue. Therefore, the political risks associated with these areas continue to exist and add to investor uncertainty. In addition, with oil prices exceeding $100 per barrel, inflationary pressures remain alive and well.

Therefore, operationally I am reducing my equity allocation in my flexible portfolios from 100% to 75%. In particular, I am selling some of my exposure to the semiconductor and memory sectors due to their extreme volatility. However, I am retaining my exposures to profitable software companies whose share prices tend to be less volatile than the aforementioned sectors. Furthermore, I am adding healthcare/pharmaceutical companies and consumer staples with lower P/E ratios to my portfolios.

## The Bond Market Is Back In Control

There were a number of reasons why investors believed they could ignore rising interest rates. First, if rising rates were due to a strong economy then we should expect good earnings reports. Second, if there are good earnings reports, we can justify high P/E ratios. And third, the continued excitement about AI would overshadow anything else going on in the markets.

The problem is that the valuation argument becomes less comfortable when rates rise quickly and for inflation-related reasons. That is to say, when rising rates occur simultaneously with increasing oil prices, inflation, deficits, new debt issuance, political instability, and the potential for more aggressive monetary policy actions by central bankers, the entire dynamic changes.

Treasury Yields at 4.6% and 5%+ yields for 30 year Treasuries are large numbers that are difficult to ignore. These represent the cost of money and affect every area of the financial world including valuations, mortgage pricing, private lending terms, share repurchases, debt service costs, business investment (CapEx) and CapEx planning. Growth stocks, which have multiple years of future cash flow that have contributed greatly to their current valuation levels, are most affected by rising discount rates. An established company earning a profit today is able to withstand a rise in discount rates better than a company whose valuation is based on future cash flows.

Growth companies, for example Nvidia, are currently experiencing growth rates far exceeding historical norms. Therefore, the ability of earnings and cash flow to grow rapidly is possible. However, this rapid growth in earnings/cash flow also creates the fact that a substantial portion of their value is tied to future cash flow rather than current cash flow.

This is where the idea of "equity duration" comes into play. A stock such as Walmart is highly valued for its current profitability. On the other hand, a growth stock is valued almost exclusively on future profitability. The more you pay for future profitability, the more susceptible your stock becomes to fluctuations in interest rates.

When valuing a company utilizing a DCF (Discounted Cash Flow) model, estimates are made regarding future cash flows and discounted back to today's value using a discount rate. NYU Professor of Finance Aswath Damodaran does an excellent job of explaining the mechanics behind DCF models. The primary components of this discount rate are cost of capital and U.S. Government bond yields. Thus, when Treasuries rise, the discount rate increases. When the discount rate increases, the value of future cash flows decreases.

Growth companies are especially vulnerable to rising discount rates and declining growth expectations. The more that future growth is assumed to exist in future periods, the greater the sensitivity of valuations to both rising discount rates and declining growth expectations.

This represents the fundamental issue facing equity investors today. While it may be correct to state "AI continues to grow," or "Nvidia continues to post incredible results," the valuation assigned to that growth becomes more vulnerable when long-term Treasury yields approach 5% instead of 4%. The market will need to reassess how much that growth is worth. Enthusiasm can support multiples for some time, but it cannot fully offset a higher discount rate. The more an investor's story depends upon projected future earnings, the more susceptible that story becomes to a rising cost of money.

## The Trump-Xi Summit

At first glance, it was supposed to be a major moment of geopolitical stabilization. However, in reality, it is my belief that the summit simply provided a mechanism to manage how each side would appear to act. It did allow both sides to avoid escalating tensions with each other. Nevertheless, it had little impact on the underlying structural issues that create concern for the markets. My view of the summit was that it was a "cool-down" meeting. That was the full extent of what the summit represented to me.

In summary, I do not believe that the summit was without merit. The summit did provide a means for preventing further escalation. Unfortunately, however, the markets viewed the summit as a potential big change and therefore had a substantial impact on sentiment.

Key outcomes (or lack thereof):

- **China-Taiwan:** China immediately elevated the rhetoric surrounding Taiwan; President Trump has yet to respond in kind. Xi Jinping again emphasized that the Taiwan question represents the central issue at hand, while Washington continues to maintain its policy of "strategic ambiguity."
- **Rare Earths:** No tangible agreements were reached regarding rare earth elements.
- **Semiconductors/Technology:** No clear agreement on export controls, chip access, supply chains, or military AI guardrails.
- **Hormuz/Iran:** While President Trump stated that China indicated a willingness to assist in regard to Iran/Hormuz, there has been no official confirmation of such assistance by China.
- **Soybeans & Boeing:** Interesting developments, but not the types of agreements that can significantly reduce the costs of global capital, ensure the security of the Strait of Hormuz, or prevent the risk of technological fragmentation.

## Valuations No Longer Have Margin For Error

What does this really boil down to? We're dealing with an overly expensive U.S. marketplace, and one where there is little room for error due to such high costs.

The S&P 500 offers an equity risk premium that is nearly equivalent to nothing in terms of returns relative to Treasuries. On the Nasdaq 100, information technology, semiconductors, and especially the Magnificent Seven, the premium is actually negative. As a practical result, many parts of the stock market now offer little or even negative earnings-yield pickup versus U.S. government bonds. That does not necessarily mean equities must fall sharply. Overpriced markets can continue to trade as overpriced for months. However, it also creates a very small buffer.

When margins of safety are so small, any number of events — inflation figures higher than anticipated, a weaker-than-expected Treasury auction, oil prices above $100 per barrel, the FOMC sounding somewhat less dovish than previously — can create enough pressure to cause weakness in the marketplace. Even if quarterly results (e.g., Nvidia's next quarterly report) were merely "very good," but not perfect by the standards set by current pricing, they could still be treated cautiously by investors, given how much good news is already reflected in prices.

This exact same scenario applies to semiconductor stocks. Hyperscalers are continuing to invest in AI, data center capacity is increasing and demand for computing power remains extremely robust. The issue here is not the story itself — it is the price paid for those stories.

The Philadelphia Semiconductor Index (SOX) is currently trading at approximately 25 times projected earnings, versus a ten-year average multiple of just under 19 times. To justify such a large premium to historical averages requires three separate items simultaneously: high growth rates, flawless execution by the companies, and stable interest rates.

## Software That Behaves Like Value

Not all technology is created equal, and during sell-offs there is a noticeable difference in how some software companies react vs. other parts of the overall technology space. During sell-off periods, some software companies will continue to go up.

The market appears to appreciate the following characteristics in this environment:

- High-margin businesses
- Recurring revenue models
- Capital-light business models
- Less reliance on inventory cycle fluctuations
- Less direct exposure to commodities, logistics, and hardware

Therefore, if interest rates increase due to fear of inflation and increases in the cost of capital, it is preferable to invest in companies that do not have to continually fund large amounts of capital expenditures to grow. Asset-light models with pricing power, customer retention, and revenue visibility are preferred.

This does not imply that you should buy every piece of software at any price; however, it does provide insight into why some technology names (software) appear to be performing defensively.

## Conclusion

The portfolio adjustments in my active and flexible portfolios follow three concepts:

1. **Less risk (less equity).** Cutting exposure to high-beta technology assets including semiconductors and memory by approximately 50%. These businesses are highly sensitive to increases in the cost of borrowing and have been driven to extreme valuations by investor enthusiasm.

2. **Lower volatility (less beta).** Maintaining a presence in technology, specifically software companies with a history of stable earnings, less cyclical operating models, less capital expenditure, and/or significant pricing power. These serve as a means of mitigating potential downside risk.

3. **Added protection (more defense).** Increasing holdings in healthcare/pharmaceuticals, which offers relatively predictable cash flows and reasonable valuations versus historical norms. Also adding a small allocation to select consumer staples stocks with lower P/E ratios, where investor expectations have been tempered.

I am not predicting an imminent sharp decline in the stock market. What is happening now is quite normal. A large advance in the markets typically results in a pause. My concern is that so many portfolios are being built on the assumption that artificial intelligence will continue to move every single asset class upward with no regard for the impact of rising oil costs, inflation, and long-term Treasury yields above 5%.

The reason I am reducing risk is not that company fundamentals have worsened. It is that the bond market has changed the price of money.
